Abstract

The PAM Observatory studies recursive language systems as phase-structured dynamical systems over a control manifold. Instead of treating language-model outputs as static artifacts, it treats recursively updated corpora as evolving semantic ensembles whose macroscopic behavior can be measured, geometrized, and probed.

The project began by introducing orthogonal observables over recursive corpus dynamics, including freeze occupancy, signature entropy, and trajectory invariance. Parameter sweeps over anchor strength, replacement fraction, and related controls revealed distinct behavioral regimes, including collapse-prone, mixed, and structurally persistent phases. Although freeze and entropy exhibited strong anticorrelation, regression-based tests suggested that they are not simple direct drivers of one another, but co-manifestations of a deeper latent regime structure.

Building on this empirical foundation, the observatory now reconstructs an intrinsic manifold geometry from observable behavior using a Fisher-type metric, geodesic distance structure, low-dimensional embedding, and curvature diagnostics. This geometry supports downstream extraction of phase structure through seam detection, seam-relative distance, and signed phase coordinates. The resulting manifold is not merely descriptive: it becomes experimentally actionable through operator-based probing, including geodesic extraction, canonical probes, scaled probes, Lazarus-regime diagnostics, and transition-rate analysis.

This layered framework reframes recursive language dynamics as systems governed by intrinsic geometric and topological organization rather than by simple autoregressive coupling alone. In this view, collapse, metastability, transition behavior, and structural persistence are not isolated phenomena, but manifestations of regime structure on an emergent manifold. The PAM Observatory therefore functions as a computational instrument for detecting, measuring, and probing phase organization in recursive generative systems.
