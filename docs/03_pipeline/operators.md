# Operators

## Overview

The PAM Observatory no longer operates purely as a descriptive system.

With the introduction of operators, it becomes an **experimental instrument**.

Operators act on trajectories within the parameter manifold, allowing the system to:
- probe structure under constraint
- induce transformations
- measure responses of the geometry

This marks the transition from:

> describing geometry → probing geometry

---

## Definition

An **operator** is a transformation acting on a trajectory in the parameter manifold.

Formally:

\[
\theta(t) \xrightarrow{S} \tilde{\theta}(t)
\]

where:
- \( \theta(t) \): original trajectory
- \( S \): operator
- \( \tilde{\theta}(t) \): transformed trajectory

Operators do not change the manifold itself —  
they change how trajectories move through it.

---

## Why Operators Matter

Before operators, the observatory could:
- map geometry
- estimate curvature
- detect phase boundaries

With operators, the observatory can:
- test how trajectories interact with structure
- induce controlled perturbations
- measure collapse, divergence, and recovery

This introduces a full experimental loop:

1. Observe → geometry pipeline  
2. Model → phase + seam + curvature  
3. Act → operator  
4. Measure → outcome metrics  

---

## Canonical Operator: Geodesic Extraction (S)

### Description

**Geodesic Extraction (S)** transforms a trajectory under manifold constraints.

It attempts to:
- align trajectories with intrinsic geometry
- reveal shortest-path behavior under the Fisher metric
- expose interaction with phase boundaries

### Role

S is the first **active probe** of the manifold.

It converts trajectories from:
- passive records

into:
- objects that can be transformed and tested

---

## Operator Effects

Applying an operator enables measurement of:

### 1. Collapse
- trajectory converges to a degenerate or invariant state
- often associated with horizon or boundary interaction

### 2. Divergence
- trajectory separates from original path
- indicates instability or high curvature

### 3. Constraint Interaction
- trajectory deforms near seam or boundary
- reveals structure of transition regions

---

## Constraint Surfaces

A key upgrade introduced by operators:

> The phase boundary is no longer just a seam —  
> it becomes a **dynamical constraint surface**.

Operators allow trajectories to:
- approach the boundary
- compress against it
- cross it (or fail to)

This turns static geometry into observable dynamics.

---

## Event Classes (Emerging)

Operators make discrete event types observable.

### Seam Crossing
- trajectory transitions between phase regions

### Lazarus Regime
- high-pressure pre-collapse zone
- trajectory compresses before either collapsing or recovering

### Collapse (“Brick”)
- trajectory degenerates into minimal or non-informative output
- treated as a measurable event (not noise)

---

## Operators as Algebra

The system is evolving from:

- data + geometry

to:

- transformations on trajectories

This introduces the beginnings of an **operator algebra**:

- operators act on trajectories
- outputs can be composed, compared, and classified

Future work may include:
- operator composition
- operator equivalence
- operator-induced invariants

---

## Invariance Perspective

Operators help expose a key invariant of the system:

> **Topology is preserved under representation, but revealed through transformation.**

While embeddings and visualizations may vary:
- operator outcomes reveal stable structural behavior

---

## Summary

Operators turn the PAM Observatory into an active instrument.

They enable:

- controlled interaction with the manifold  
- measurement of structural response  
- identification of dynamic phenomena  

In short:

> Geometry tells you what exists.  
> Operators tell you how it behaves.
